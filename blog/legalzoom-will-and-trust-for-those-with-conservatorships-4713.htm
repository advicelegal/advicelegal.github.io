LegalZoom Will and Trust for Those with Conservatorships

Home / Blog

Navigating estate planning can be complex, especially for individuals under conservatorships. Whether due to age, disability, or other circumstances, those with conservatorships face unique legal challenges when securing their assets and ensuring their wishes are honored. LegalZoom’s will and trust services offer a streamlined solution, but how effective are they for this specific demographic?

Understanding Conservatorships and Estate Planning

A conservatorship is a legal arrangement where a court appoints a guardian (conservator) to manage the financial and personal affairs of an individual deemed unable to do so themselves. This often applies to seniors with dementia, adults with disabilities, or those recovering from severe injuries.

Why Estate Planning Matters for Conservatees

For conservatees, estate planning isn’t just about distributing assets—it’s about maintaining autonomy where possible. A well-structured will or trust can:
- Clarify wishes regarding medical care and asset distribution.
- Minimize family disputes.
- Ensure dependents or charities receive intended support.

Without proper documentation, courts may make decisions that don’t align with the conservatee’s preferences.

LegalZoom’s Role in Simplifying the Process

LegalZoom has democratized legal services by offering affordable, online-based will and trust creation. But is it suitable for conservatees?

Pros of Using LegalZoom

  1. Accessibility – Ideal for conservatees with limited mobility or those in rural areas.
  2. Cost-Effective – More affordable than hiring an estate attorney.
  3. User-Friendly – Step-by-step guidance simplifies complex legal jargon.

Potential Drawbacks

  1. Lack of Personalized Advice – Conservatorships often require nuanced legal strategies.
  2. State-Specific Variations – Laws differ by state; DIY forms may not cover all scenarios.
  3. Limited Oversight – No attorney reviews the documents unless you pay extra.

Key Considerations When Using LegalZoom

1. Assessing Mental Capacity

Courts may challenge a will or trust if the conservatee’s mental capacity is in question. LegalZoom’s platform doesn’t evaluate this—consulting an attorney is wise.

2. Coordinating with the Conservator

The conservator should be involved in the process to ensure compliance with court orders and avoid conflicts.

3. Choosing Between a Will and a Trust

  • Will – Best for straightforward estates but requires probate.
  • Trust – Avoids probate, offers privacy, and can manage assets during incapacity.

Real-World Implications

Case Study: A Conservatee’s Success Story

Jane, a 68-year-old with early-stage Alzheimer’s, used LegalZoom to create a living trust with her conservator’s help. The process allowed her to:
- Designate her son as successor trustee.
- Specify care preferences for her pet.
- Avoid probate delays after her passing.

When LegalZoom Might Not Be Enough

Complex estates, blended families, or disputes over conservatorship may require an attorney’s intervention.

The Future of Digital Estate Planning

With rising conservatorship cases (especially post-pandemic), demand for accessible legal tools will grow. LegalZoom fills a gap, but hybrid models (online forms + attorney review) may become the gold standard.

Final Thoughts

For conservatees, LegalZoom offers a practical starting point, but it’s not a one-size-fits-all solution. Balancing convenience with legal safeguards is key—because everyone deserves a say in their legacy.
