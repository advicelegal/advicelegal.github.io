India’s LGBTQ+ Soldiers: Why They Deserve Marriage Rights

Home / Blog

The fight for LGBTQ+ rights in India has seen significant milestones, from the decriminalization of homosexuality in 2018 to ongoing battles for marriage equality. Yet, one group remains largely overlooked in these discussions: LGBTQ+ soldiers serving in the Indian Armed Forces. These individuals dedicate their lives to protecting the nation, yet they are denied the basic right to marry the person they love. In a country where military service is revered, it’s time to ask: Why shouldn’t India’s LGBTQ+ soldiers have the same marital rights as their heterosexual counterparts?

The Silent Struggle of LGBTQ+ Soldiers

India’s armed forces are among the largest in the world, with over 1.4 million active personnel. Yet, the institution remains deeply conservative, adhering to traditions that often exclude LGBTQ+ individuals. While there is no explicit ban on LGBTQ+ people serving, the lack of legal recognition for same-sex marriages creates an invisible barrier. Soldiers in same-sex relationships cannot access spousal benefits, joint housing, or even public acknowledgment of their partnerships.

The Hypocrisy of "Service Before Self"

The Indian military’s core values emphasize "Service Before Self," yet this principle is selectively applied. Heterosexual soldiers enjoy the right to marry, receive housing allowances for their families, and access healthcare for their spouses. LGBTQ+ soldiers, however, are expected to serve with the same dedication while being denied these fundamental rights. This hypocrisy undermines the very ideals the armed forces claim to uphold.

Imagine a scenario where a gay soldier is deployed to a conflict zone. His partner has no legal standing to be notified in case of an emergency, nor can they access military healthcare or survivor benefits. This isn’t just unfair—it’s a blatant disregard for the sacrifices these soldiers make.

The Global Context: Progress Elsewhere

India is not alone in grappling with this issue, but many nations have taken steps forward. The United States, for example, lifted its "Don’t Ask, Don’t Tell" policy in 2011, allowing LGBTQ+ soldiers to serve openly. In 2022, President Biden signed an executive order recognizing same-sex marriages for military personnel, ensuring equal access to benefits. Similarly, the UK, Canada, and Israel have integrated LGBTQ+ rights into their military policies.

If these countries can modernize their armed forces without compromising discipline or morale, why can’t India? The argument that LGBTQ+ rights would disrupt military cohesion is outdated and disproven by global examples.

The Economic and Social Benefits

Granting marriage rights to LGBTQ+ soldiers isn’t just about equality—it’s also practical. Studies show that inclusive policies improve morale, retention, and recruitment. When soldiers feel accepted, they perform better. Moreover, extending spousal benefits to same-sex partners would stimulate local economies near military bases, as families spend on housing, education, and healthcare.

The Legal Battle Ahead

India’s Supreme Court is currently deliberating on marriage equality, and the outcome could redefine LGBTQ+ rights nationwide. However, even if the court rules in favor, the armed forces may resist change without explicit policy reforms. The government must take proactive steps to ensure that military regulations align with constitutional principles of equality and non-discrimination.

The Role of Leadership

Military leaders have a responsibility to foster inclusivity. Silence on LGBTQ+ issues perpetuates stigma. By publicly supporting marriage rights, senior officers could shift the culture within the ranks. Veterans’ associations and advocacy groups must also amplify the voices of LGBTQ+ soldiers, ensuring their stories are heard.

A Call to Action

India’s LGBTQ+ soldiers deserve more than gratitude—they deserve equality. The right to marry is not a privilege; it’s a human right. As the nation progresses toward greater inclusivity, the armed forces must not be left behind. The time for change is now.

The next time you see a soldier in uniform, ask yourself: Shouldn’t their love be as respected as their service?
