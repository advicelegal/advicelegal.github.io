How to Document Your Legal Internship Experience for Future Jobs

Home / Blog

Landing a legal internship is a significant milestone, but making the most of it requires careful documentation. Whether you’re interning at a corporate law firm, a nonprofit, or a government agency, your experience can be a powerful asset for future job applications. Here’s how to document your legal internship effectively, ensuring it stands out in a competitive job market.

Why Documenting Your Internship Matters

In today’s fast-paced legal industry, employers look for candidates with hands-on experience and tangible skills. Simply listing "Legal Intern" on your resume won’t cut it—you need to showcase what you accomplished and how it prepared you for future roles. Proper documentation helps you:

  • Stand out in job applications by providing concrete examples of your work.
  • Prepare for interviews with detailed anecdotes about your contributions.
  • Build a professional portfolio for networking or graduate school applications.
  • Track your growth to identify strengths and areas for improvement.

Step 1: Keep a Detailed Work Journal

What to Record Daily

Start by maintaining a daily or weekly journal. Include:
- Tasks you performed: Research, drafting memos, attending client meetings, etc.
- Skills you developed: Legal writing, negotiation, case analysis, etc.
- Challenges you faced: How you resolved them or what you learned.
- Feedback received: Notes from supervisors or mentors.

Use a Structured Format

Organize your journal with headings like:
- Date/Week
- Key Projects
- Lessons Learned
- Supervisor Feedback

This makes it easier to reference later when updating your resume or preparing for interviews.

Step 2: Save Samples of Your Work (When Permitted)

Types of Documents to Collect

If your internship allows, save anonymized versions of:
- Legal memos or briefs you drafted.
- Research summaries or case analyses.
- Presentations or reports you contributed to.

How to Organize Them

Create a digital folder with subfolders for:
- Writing Samples
- Research Projects
- Meeting Notes
- Feedback/Evaluations

Label files clearly (e.g., "DraftMemoContractDispute2023").

Step 3: Quantify Your Achievements

Employers love numbers. Where possible, quantify your contributions:
- "Researched and summarized 20+ cases on intellectual property law."
- "Drafted 5 client advisories on regulatory compliance."
- "Assisted in preparing trial exhibits for a $2M commercial dispute."

Even small metrics can make your experience sound more impactful.

Step 4: Seek Feedback and Testimonials

Ask for Formal Evaluations

Many internships include evaluations—request a copy for your records. If not, ask your supervisor for written feedback.

Request LinkedIn Recommendations

A short testimonial like, "Jane’s research skills were invaluable to our team," adds credibility to your profile.

Keep a "Brag Sheet"

Compile positive feedback in one document. For example:
- "Supervisor praised my attention to detail in contract review."
- "Mentor noted my strong analytical skills during case prep."

Step 5: Update Your Resume and LinkedIn

Tailor Your Resume

Use action verbs and specifics:
- Instead of: "Worked on legal research."
- Write: "Conducted in-depth legal research on employment discrimination cases, resulting in a 10-page memo cited by senior attorneys."

Enhance Your LinkedIn Profile

  • Add the internship under "Experience" with bullet points mirroring your resume.
  • Use the "Featured" section to upload work samples (if public-friendly).
  • Join relevant groups (e.g., "Young Lawyers Network") to stay connected.

Step 6: Reflect on Your Experience

Identify Key Takeaways

Ask yourself:
- What did I enjoy most? (e.g., litigation, transactional work)
- What skills do I want to improve? (e.g., oral advocacy, contract drafting)
- How does this align with my career goals?

Adjust Your Career Plan Accordingly

If you loved corporate law, seek similar roles. If you disliked courtroom work, pivot toward compliance or policy.

Bonus: Leverage Your Experience in Interviews

When asked, "Tell me about your internship," structure your answer with:
1. Context: Where you interned and the team’s focus.
2. Role: Your specific responsibilities.
3. Achievements: What you accomplished (use numbers!).
4. Lessons: How it shaped your career goals.

Example:
"At XYZ Law, I supported the litigation team by researching case law and drafting motions. I analyzed 15+ precedents for a high-profile fraud case, and my memo was used in settlement negotiations. This confirmed my passion for dispute resolution."

Final Tips

  • Network before you leave: Connect with colleagues on LinkedIn and ask for advice.
  • Follow up: Send thank-you emails referencing specific projects you worked on.
  • Stay organized: Update your documentation regularly—don’t wait until job-hunting season.

By meticulously documenting your legal internship, you’ll turn a short-term opportunity into a long-term career advantage. Future employers won’t just see an intern—they’ll see a skilled, proactive professional ready to contribute from day one.
