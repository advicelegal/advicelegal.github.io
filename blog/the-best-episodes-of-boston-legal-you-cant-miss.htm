The Best Episodes of Boston Legal You Can’t Miss

Home / Blog

Boston Legal, the brilliant spin-off of The Practice, was never just a television show. It was a sharp, witty, and often profoundly moving social commentary disguised as a legal dramedy. Through the exploits of the eccentric lawyers at the firm of Crane, Poole & & Schmidt, particularly the legendary duo of Alan Shore and Denny Crane, the show tackled some of the most contentious issues of its time with unparalleled audacity and heart. What is truly astonishing, however, is how prescient it remains. The cases and conversations that unfolded in those hallowed halls and balconies are not relics of the mid-2000s; they are a mirror reflecting our current, turbulent world. If you're looking to understand the present through the lens of this masterpiece, these are the episodes you absolutely cannot miss.

The Unwavering Relevance of a Legal Comedy

Before diving into the specific episodes, it's crucial to understand why Boston Legal retains its power. David E. Kelley’s writing was fearless. He used the legal framework not just to present "cases of the week," but to stage philosophical debates about morality, justice, and the American soul. The show was a sanctuary for radical honesty, where characters could voice uncomfortable truths, often breaking the fourth wall to do so. In an era of echo chambers and polarized discourse, the show's commitment to exploring all sides of an argument—even the ones it ultimately disagreed with—feels like a breath of fresh, if sometimes contentious, air.

The Pillars of the Show: Shore and Crane

At its core, the show's magic was alchemical, born from the relationship between Alan Shore (James Spader) and Denny Crane (William Shatner). Alan, the morally flexible liberal with a golden heart and a silver tongue, and Denny, the staunchly conservative, narcissistic, and yet lovable gun enthusiast, should have been enemies. Instead, they were the deepest of friends. Their nightly balcony scenes, Scotch in hand, were the soul of the series. They modeled a form of friendship that transcended politics—a concept that feels increasingly rare today. They debated everything from war to women's rights, but their bond was unbreakable. In our age of "cancel culture" and fractured relationships over political differences, their friendship is a poignant reminder of what can be found in common humanity.

Episodes That Echo Into Today's Headlines

Here are the essential episodes that speak directly to the defining issues of our modern world.

1. "Son of the Defender" (Season 2, Episode 2 & 3)

The Modern Hot-Button Issue: Cancel Culture, Public Shaming, and the Court of Public Opinion

This powerful two-part episode sees Alan Shore defending a deeply unpopular client: a teenage boy accused of murdering a 9-year-old girl. The town is gripped by a media frenzy, and the public has already tried and convicted the boy in the court of public opinion. The case becomes less about the forensic evidence and more about managing the overwhelming tide of public hatred and media bias.

In today's world, where social media can destroy lives and careers in an instant based on allegations, "Son of the Defender" is a stunningly relevant exploration of the principle "innocent until proven guilty." Alan's closing argument is a masterpiece, a blistering critique of mob mentality. He argues that the public's desire for a villain, for a simple narrative, is a greater threat to justice than any single alleged crime. He defends the very foundation of the legal system against the tyranny of popular opinion—a battle that is fought daily on platforms like Twitter and Facebook. This episode is a must-watch for anyone grappling with the complex interplay between justice, media, and public sentiment in the digital age.

2. "The Cancer Man Can" (Season 3, Episode 9)

The Modern Hot-Button Issue: Big Pharma, Healthcare Costs, and Corporate Greed

Denny Crane, fearing he has Mad Cow Disease, is instead diagnosed with a treatable, slow-progressing form of prostate cancer. However, the episode's central case involves a major pharmaceutical company. The firm is being sued for withholding a cheaper, effective cancer drug from the market to protect the profits of their far more expensive treatment.

The parallels to contemporary debates about healthcare and pharmaceutical monopolies are undeniable. The episode delves into the moral bankruptcy of prioritizing shareholder value over human life. It questions a system where the ability to pay determines the right to live. With insulin prices and the cost of life-saving drugs still making headlines, "The Cancer Man Can" is a raw and angry look at a system that too often sees patients as profit centers. It’s a story about the human cost of unregulated capitalism, a theme that has only grown more urgent.

3. "Trick or Treat" (Season 2, Episode 7)

The Modern Hot-Button Issue: LGBTQ+ Rights, Religious Freedom, and Discrimination

On Halloween, Alan takes on the case of a gay man who was denied a promotion at a private, religious school because of his sexuality. The school claims its religious freedoms protect it from anti-discrimination lawsuits. This case perfectly encapsulates the ongoing legal and cultural battle between religious liberty and LGBTQ+ rights, a conflict that continues to be fought in courtrooms and legislatures across the United States.

Alan's argument is not just a legal one; it's a moral and human one. He separates the teachings of Christ from the bigotry of man, arguing that using religion as a shield for discrimination is a perversion of faith. The emotional weight of the episode comes from the plaintiff's genuine love for his job and his students, juxtaposed with the cold, institutional prejudice he faces. In a world where "religious freedom" laws are often used to justify discrimination, this episode remains a powerful and eloquent plea for equality.

4. "Smile" (Season 4, Episode 13)

The Modern Hot-Button Issue: Immigration, Nationalism, and Human Dignity

In one of the show's most heart-wrenching storylines, Denise Bauer defends a Mexican family facing deportation. The case highlights the brutal inefficiencies and human tragedies of the U.S. immigration system. The family, who has built a life in America, is torn apart by a rigid and unforgiving application of the law.

"Smile" forces the audience to look beyond political slogans and see the human faces behind the immigration debate. It tackles themes of nationalism, fear of the "other," and the meaning of the American dream. The episode doesn't offer easy solutions, but it compellingly argues that a nation's character is judged by how it treats the most vulnerable within its borders. With immigration remaining one of the most divisive global issues, this episode's compassionate perspective is more necessary than ever.

5. "The Mighty Rogues" (Season 5, Episode 11)

The Modern Hot-Button Issue: Gun Control and the Second Amendment

It's impossible to discuss Boston Legal without addressing Denny Crane's other great love: his gun. This episode features a case that strikes at the very heart of the Second Amendment debate. While the specific legal details are fantastical (involving a lawsuit against a video game company), the core of the episode is a profound debate between Alan and Denny about the role of guns in American society.

Denny embodies the absolutist view of the Second Amendment, seeing guns as a fundamental right and a necessary tool for self-preservation. Alan, while respecting his friend, presents the liberal counter-argument, focusing on the societal cost of widespread gun ownership. The brilliance of the episode is that it doesn't devolve into a shouting match. It's a thoughtful, character-driven exploration of a deeply entrenched American divide. In a country where mass shootings are tragically routine, the nuanced, albeit passionate, conversation between these two friends offers a more productive model for discussion than what we typically see on cable news.

Beyond the Cases: The Balcony as a Sanctuary for Civil Discourse

The true genius of Boston Legal was that it never let the legal verdict have the final word. The final word always belonged to Alan and Denny on the balcony. These scenes were the decompression chamber for the week's moral and ethical turmoil. They were where the show acknowledged that the law is imperfect, that justice is not always served in the courtroom, and that friendship is the ultimate defense against a chaotic world.

In our current climate, where civil discourse is often the first casualty of political disagreement, the balcony stands as a monument to a different way. It was a space where two men from diametrically opposed worldviews could not only coexist but find solace, understanding, and love in each other's company. They listened to each other, even when they disagreed. They found common ground in their shared fears, their love of Scotch, and their unwavering loyalty.

Watching Boston Legal today is not just an exercise in nostalgia. It is an invitation to engage with the most difficult questions of our time through a lens of intelligence, humor, and, above all, humanity. These episodes provide not just entertainment, but a framework for thought. They remind us that while the law is a powerful tool, it is our capacity for empathy, our courage to defend the unpopular, and our ability to maintain friendships across ideological chasms that will ultimately define our future. So, pour a glass of Scotch (or your beverage of choice), find these episodes, and prepare for a show that, like a fine single malt, has only gotten richer and more complex with age.
